I finally got LEDs to have consistent brightness across every single display. This MAX6971ANG (https://www.mouser.com/datasheet/2/609/MAX6971-3130829.pdf) LED driver was something that we attempted to use early on, but couldn’t quite figure out why it didn’t work. I started with where we left off and tried a few different approaches. I added a decoupling capacitor to the voltage input and had to readjust our SPI test code to get it to work with the latch timing of this chip to start out with, but that didn’t seem to work. I then used an oscilloscope to try and gleam some kind of information about the inputs and outputs of the signals coming from the driver. One thing I figured out was that the outputs are being driven out correctly by the chip, but the LEDs just weren’t turning on. The voltage reading on the oscilloscope would go from HI-Z to 0V and that told me that some kind of output was being driven. I figured out that the chip was just outputting current and not voltage. So, I added some pull up resistors and that turned on the LEDs, but that was only for the LED selector side of the pins because the segment selectors worked just fine without any voltage. This reduced the number of ICs from 5 in the previous design down to just this one, but that also renders the SPI PCBs completely useless. Going to look into a protoboard design for something like this since it is literally just a set of inputs into one IC and then outputs.

 

Cameron and I cut out the edges for the rails to sit in. He used a circular saw to cut through the edges and I held a vacuum up to collect any saw dust in the air. Sparks flew around when he hit a staple, but it worked out fine. We used a chisel and a rounder to flatten out the cuts and smooth everything out. We did encounter an issue with the side of the rail coming loose, so I just put some wood glue on there to hold it in place.
